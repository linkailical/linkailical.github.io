The Digital SAT tests **vocabulary in context**, not obscure memorization. These are common, "academic-tone" words whose *exact shade of meaning* matters. For each: meaning + an example in SAT style. Cover the meaning and predict from the sentence first.

---

## Describing arguments & ideas

- **substantiate** (v.) — to support with evidence. *The study substantiates the claim that sleep aids memory.*
- **undermine** (v.) — to weaken. *New data undermine the older theory.*
- **nuanced** (adj.) — having subtle distinctions. *Her nuanced argument avoided simple labels.*
- **tenuous** (adj.) — weak, flimsy. *The link between the two events is tenuous.*
- **compelling** (adj.) — convincing, forceful. *The evidence is compelling.*
- **speculative** (adj.) — based on guessing, not proof. *His conclusion is speculative.*
- **empirical** (adj.) — based on observation/experiment. *Empirical data support the model.*
- **refute** (v.) — to prove false. *The experiment refutes the hypothesis.*

## Describing tone & attitude

- **measured** (adj.) — careful and restrained. *A measured response, neither angry nor dismissive.*
- **ambivalent** (adj.) — having mixed feelings. *She is ambivalent about the change.*
- **skeptical** (adj.) — doubtful. *Readers remained skeptical of the bold claim.*
- **candid** (adj.) — honest and direct. *A candid admission of error.*
- **indifferent** (adj.) — not caring. *He was indifferent to the outcome.*
- **earnest** (adj.) — sincere and serious. *An earnest plea for support.*

## Change & process

- **mitigate** (v.) — to make less severe. *Planting trees can mitigate flooding.*
- **exacerbate** (v.) — to make worse. *The delay exacerbated the shortage.*
- **proliferate** (v.) — to increase rapidly. *Apps proliferated after the launch.*
- **diminish** (v.) — to reduce. *Interest diminished over time.*
- **catalyst** (n.) — something that speeds change. *The report was a catalyst for reform.*
- **transient** (adj.) — short-lived. *A transient improvement.*

## Useful all-purpose academic words

- **plausible** (adj.) — believable. *A plausible explanation.*
- **arbitrary** (adj.) — based on whim, not reason. *An arbitrary rule.*
- **autonomous** (adj.) — independent, self-governing. *An autonomous region.*
- **paradox** (n.) — a seeming contradiction. *The paradox of choice.*
- **scrutiny** (n.) — close examination. *The plan faced scrutiny.*
- **prevalent** (adj.) — widespread. *The view is prevalent among experts.*
- **viable** (adj.) — workable. *A viable alternative.*
- **discern** (v.) — to perceive/distinguish. *Hard to discern a pattern.*
- **novel** (adj.) — new, original. *A novel approach.* (Note: also a noun = a book.)
- **redundant** (adj.) — needlessly repetitive. *Cut the redundant phrase.*

## Self-quiz (cover the words above)

1. To make a problem worse = ______
2. Believable but not proven = ______
3. To support with evidence = ______
4. Short-lived = ______
5. Mixed feelings = ______

> **Answers:** 1. exacerbate 2. plausible (or speculative) 3. substantiate 4. transient 5. ambivalent.

**How to study:** when you meet an unknown word in practice, don't just memorize a translation — record the **sentence** it came from. SAT meaning is always context-driven.
