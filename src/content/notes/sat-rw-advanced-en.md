The hardest Reading & Writing questions reward a precise process, not speed-reading. Here are the high-difficulty types and exactly how to attack each.

---

## 1. Cross-text connections (paired passages)

Process: (1) summarize Text 1's claim + stance in your own words; (2) do the same for Text 2; (3) define the relationship (agree / disagree / extend / qualify / give evidence for or against); (4) answer literally what's asked ("how would author 2 respond to author 1's view that …").

**Trap:** picking a choice that is true about one text but doesn't capture the *relationship*.

**Practice.** Text 1: standardized tests predict college grades well. Text 2: prediction is weak once high-school GPA is included. Author 2 would most likely view Text 1's claim as:
(A) confirmed by additional data.
(B) overstated because it ignores GPA's role. ✅
(C) irrelevant to admissions.
(D) too cautious.

## 2. Command of evidence — quantitative (graphs/tables)

Process: read the **title, axes, and units** first; find the specific data point the claim needs; make sure the choice is both **true to the data** and **relevant to the claim**.

**Trap:** a choice that states a correct number from the graph but doesn't support the sentence's claim.

**Practice.** A claim says a city's bus ridership "rose every year from 2018–2022." Which finding from the chart would **support** it?
(A) Ridership was highest in 2018.
(B) Ridership increased each year, reaching its peak in 2022. ✅
(C) Ridership fell in 2020.
(D) Ridership was flat.

## 3. Rhetorical synthesis (the "student's goal" task)

Process: read the **goal sentence first** (it's the rubric). Then pick the option that (a) achieves *that* goal and (b) uses the given notes accurately. Ignore fluent options that miss the goal.

**Common goals:** emphasize a contrast/similarity; introduce a topic to a non-expert; present a specific number; generalize from examples.

**Practice.** Goal: *introduce the study's main finding to a general audience.*
(A) "The ANOVA yielded p < 0.01 across conditions."
(B) "The study found that students who slept more scored higher on memory tests." ✅
(C) "Future work should expand the sample."
(D) "The researchers used a within-subjects design."

## 4. Transitions — the precise relationship

Don't choose by "sound." Name the logical link, then match:
- Cause/effect: *therefore, thus, consequently* — vs — Contrast: *however, nevertheless* — vs — Concession then reversal: *still, even so*.

**Trap:** two transition choices feel similar (e.g., *however* vs *for example*) — only one matches the actual logic.

**Practice.** "The drug reduced symptoms in trials. ______, it caused side effects in some patients."
(A) Therefore  (B) For example  (C) However ✅  (D) Likewise

## 5. The hardest punctuation cases

- **Colon `:`** needs a **complete sentence before it**; it introduces a list/explanation. *"She had one goal: to win."*
- **Semicolon `;`** joins **two complete sentences** (or separates list items that contain commas).
- **No comma** between a verb and its object, or before an **essential** ("restrictive") clause: *"Students who study pass."* (no commas around *who study*).
- **Nonessential** info takes a comma pair: *"My coach, who is strict, helped me."*

**Practice.** "The lab announced a breakthrough ______ a cheaper way to purify water."
(A) , (comma)  (B) : ✅  (C) ;  (D) , and

> **Why:** a complete sentence precedes, and what follows explains/specifies the breakthrough → colon. A semicolon (C) fails because the second part is not a complete sentence.

## 6. Inference / "most logically completes"

Pick the **smallest** conclusion the text forces. Eliminate choices that add new information, go extreme, or only restate a detail.

**Practice.** "The fossils were found in rock layers far older than any previously known for the species; this suggests the species ______"
(A) is now extinct.
(B) existed much earlier than scientists had thought. ✅
(C) lived only in one region.
(D) was larger than relatives.

## Drill (mixed, harder)

**D1.** "Although the theory is elegant, ______ it has not been confirmed by experiment."
(A) therefore  (B) it  (C) however  (D) so

**D2.** Goal: *emphasize a contrast between the two artists' styles.* Choose the option type you'd pick (describe in one line).

**D3.** "The garden features three colors of roses ______ red, white, and yellow."
(A) ,  (B) :  (C) ;  (D) and

> **Answers.** D1 **B** — "Although …, [subject] …" needs the clause's subject, not another connector. D2 — choose the option that explicitly states a *difference* (e.g., "whereas X is bold, Y is restrained"). D3 **B** — a complete sentence precedes; a colon introduces the list.
