## Grammar (Standard English Conventions)

**Joining clauses (boundaries).** Two independent clauses may be joined by:
- a period `.`  |  a semicolon `;`  |  a colon `:` (when the second explains the first)  |  a comma **+ FANBOYS** (for, and, nor, but, or, yet, so).
- A comma **alone** = comma splice (wrong). A subordinate clause (because, although, when, if…) attaches with a comma or no comma but is **not** independent.

**Punctuation quick rules**
- **Dependent + Independent:** "Although it rained, we played." (comma after the intro clause)
- **Nonessential info** is set off by a pair of commas, dashes, or parentheses: "My brother, a doctor, called."
- **Colon** needs a complete sentence before it. **Semicolon** needs complete sentences on both sides.
- **No comma** between subject and verb, or before an essential phrase.

**Agreement & forms**
- **Subject–verb:** ignore words between subject and verb. *"The set of tools is…"*
- **Pronoun:** match number (singular ↔ singular) and use clear antecedents; *its* (possessive) vs *it's* (it is); *their/there/they're*.
- **Verb tense:** match the passage's timeframe; keep parallel verbs consistent.
- **Modifiers:** an opening modifier describes the noun right after the comma (no dangling modifiers).
- **Parallelism:** items in a list/comparison share the same grammatical form.

## Expression of Ideas

**Transitions — name the relationship first, then choose:**
- Contrast: *however, but, yet, nevertheless, on the other hand, in contrast*
- Cause/effect: *therefore, thus, as a result, consequently, so*
- Addition: *moreover, furthermore, in addition, also*
- Example: *for example, for instance*
- Sequence: *first, then, finally, subsequently*
- Emphasis/restate: *indeed, in fact*

**Rhetorical synthesis.** Read the **goal** in the prompt first (e.g., "emphasize a contrast," "introduce the topic to an unfamiliar audience"). Choose the option that fulfills exactly that goal using the bullet notes; ignore fluent but off-goal options.

## Craft and Structure

- **Words in context:** predict your own word from the sentence; plug each choice back; beware secondary meanings of common words.
- **Text structure & purpose:** why does this sentence/paragraph exist? (define, illustrate, contrast, qualify, counter).
- **Cross-text connections:** state each author's claim + stance, then their relationship (agree / disagree / extend / qualify). Find what one author would say about the other's point.

## Information and Ideas

- **Central idea:** supported by the whole passage, not one detail.
- **Command of evidence (text):** the choice the passage directly backs.
- **Command of evidence (quantitative):** read the graph/table — axes, units, trend — and pick the data that supports the claim.
- **Inference / "most logically completes":** the smallest conclusion the text forces; avoid extremes unless the text is that strong.

## Universal strategy

1. Read the **question type** first; for grammar, let the rule decide, not what "sounds right."
2. For reading, **predict from the text**, then eliminate choices that are true-but-unsupported, too extreme, or off-topic.
3. Two choices left? Find the **specific word** that makes one wrong.
