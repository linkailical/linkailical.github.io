The Digital SAT Reading and Writing section has **54 questions in 64 minutes** (two modules of 27 questions). Each question has **one short passage** and **four answer choices**. Questions appear roughly in order of the four domains below. The single most useful habit: **decide your own answer from the text first, then match it to a choice** — and eliminate choices that are true in general but unsupported by the passage.

---

## 1. Information and Ideas

*Central ideas, details, command of evidence (text and graphs), inferences.*

**Strategies**

- **Main idea:** the choice the *whole* passage supports, not just one line.
- **Evidence / data:** the answer must be directly supported by the passage or the chart; check axes, units, and trends before choosing.
- **Inference / "logically completes":** pick the conclusion the passage *forces*, the smallest safe step — avoid extreme words (always, never) unless the text is that strong.

**Practice 1.1.** A passage explains that a plant species opens its flowers only at night and is pollinated by moths. Which choice best states the main idea?
(A) Moths are the only nocturnal pollinators.
(B) The plant's night-blooming aligns with its moth pollinators. ✅
(C) The plant cannot survive without sunlight.
(D) Moths prefer this plant to all others.

> **Why:** (B) ties the two stated facts together. (A), (C), (D) overreach beyond the text.

**Practice 1.2 (logical completion).** "Early critics dismissed the novel as shapeless. Recent scholars, however, argue its structure is carefully designed; therefore the novel is best understood as ______"
(A) genuinely formless.
(B) more deliberately constructed than once believed. ✅
(C) impossible to interpret.
(D) unpopular with readers.

> **Why:** "however" + "carefully designed" signals a contrast with the early view → (B).

## 2. Craft and Structure

*Words in context, text structure and purpose, cross-text connections.*

**Strategies**

- **Words in context:** cover the choices, predict your own word from the sentence, then match. Test each option back in the sentence.
- **Purpose:** ask *why* the author wrote this sentence/paragraph (to illustrate, contrast, qualify, define).
- **Cross-text (paired passages):** first nail each author's claim and stance, then their relationship (agree, disagree, extend, qualify).

**Practice 2.1 (word in context).** "The committee's response was ______: it neither approved nor rejected the plan, leaving everyone uncertain."
(A) decisive
(B) noncommittal ✅
(C) hostile
(D) generous

> **Why:** "neither approved nor rejected" defines the blank → (B).

**Practice 2.2 (purpose).** An author describes a failed early experiment before presenting a successful later one. The first paragraph most likely serves to:
(A) prove the later experiment was unnecessary.
(B) provide background that makes the later success meaningful. ✅
(C) argue experiments usually fail.
(D) criticize the scientist.

## 3. Expression of Ideas

*Rhetorical synthesis and transitions.*

**Strategies**

- **Rhetorical synthesis ("the student wants to…"):** read the **goal** first, then choose the option that achieves exactly that goal using the given notes — ignore stylistic flourish that misses the goal.
- **Transitions:** identify the logical relationship between the two sentences, then pick the connector.
  - Contrast: *however, but, yet, on the other hand*
  - Cause/effect: *therefore, thus, as a result, consequently*
  - Addition: *moreover, in addition, furthermore*
  - Example: *for example, for instance*

**Practice 3.1 (transition).** "The new policy reduced wait times. ______, customer complaints dropped sharply."
(A) Nevertheless
(B) However
(C) As a result ✅
(D) In contrast

> **Why:** the second sentence is an effect of the first → cause/effect connector.

**Practice 3.2 (synthesis).** Goal: *emphasize a similarity between two cities.* Best choice:
(A) "City A is older than City B."
(B) "Like City A, City B grew rapidly after building its port." ✅
(C) "City B has more residents."
(D) "City A is colder than City B."

> **Why:** only (B) states a similarity, matching the goal.

## 4. Standard English Conventions

*Punctuation/boundaries and grammar (form, structure, sense).*

**Key rules**

- **Independent clauses** can be joined by: a period; a semicolon `;`; or a comma **+** FANBOYS (for, and, nor, but, or, yet, so). A comma alone is a **comma splice** (wrong).
- **Subject–verb agreement:** match the verb to the true subject, ignoring words between them. *"The box of nails **is** heavy."*
- **Pronoun agreement:** singular antecedent → singular pronoun.
- **Verb tense:** stay consistent with the passage's timeframe.
- **Modifiers:** an opening modifier must describe the noun right after the comma (avoid dangling modifiers).
- **Apostrophes:** *its* = possessive, *it's* = "it is"; plural ≠ possessive.

**Practice 4.1 (boundaries).** "The storm knocked out power ______ residents waited hours for repairs."
(A) , (comma alone)
(B) ; ✅
(C) , and additionally
(D) and, so

> **Why:** two independent clauses need a semicolon (or comma + *and*). (A) is a comma splice.

**Practice 4.2 (agreement).** "The list of approved vendors ______ updated every month."
(A) are
(B) were
(C) is ✅
(D) have been

> **Why:** the subject is *list* (singular), not *vendors* → *is*.

**Practice 4.3 (modifier).** "Walking to school, ______"
(A) the rain started to fall.
(B) Maria felt the first drops of rain. ✅
(C) it was raining on Maria.
(D) the umbrella was forgotten.

> **Why:** the one *walking* must follow the comma → *Maria*.
