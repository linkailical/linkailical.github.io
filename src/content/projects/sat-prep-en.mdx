## Project Goal

This project turns SAT preparation into three clear questions students need to answer first: how the SAT works, what the score is used for, and how to review effectively. The goal is not to collect random resources. It is to place the test structure, score strategy, and weekly study actions on one page so students can start quickly and adjust the plan around their own target score.

To keep the material organized, the site separates the project story from the learning resources. This page explains the purpose, design, and method of the study system. The [`SAT Study Hub`](/sat/) is the knowledge entry point that links Reading & Writing, Math, problem sets, advanced topics, vocabulary, and timed practice.

## What the SAT Is

The SAT is a standardized college admissions test administered by College Board. It is mainly used for U.S. undergraduate admissions, and it can also support scholarship applications, school planning, and academic advising. A score does not decide admission by itself, but it can sit beside GPA, course rigor, activities, essays, and recommendations to show college readiness in reading, writing, and math.

The current SAT is digital. Students test in the Bluebook app, usually at an official test center or during an in-school test day. Before test day, students need to prepare their device, admission ticket, and acceptable photo ID.

## How the SAT Works

The SAT takes 2 hours and 14 minutes and includes 98 questions. It has two sections, Reading and Writing and Math. Each section has two modules, with a 10-minute break between the sections.

| Section | Time | Questions | Main Focus |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Reading and Writing | 64 minutes, two 32-minute modules | 54 questions | Reading, vocabulary in context, grammar, expression, revision |
| Math | 70 minutes, two 35-minute modules | 44 questions | Algebra, advanced math, data analysis, geometry, trigonometry |
| Total | 134 minutes | 98 questions | Digital adaptive modules |

Adaptive testing means the first module in each section contains a range of difficulty levels, and performance on that first module affects the difficulty of the second module. The first module should not be treated as a warm-up. Students need stable pacing from the start.

At the module level, each Reading and Writing module has 27 questions in 32 minutes (about 71 seconds per question), and each Math module has 22 questions in 35 minutes (about 95 seconds per question). A small number of questions are unscored "pretest" items mixed in; students cannot tell which, so every question should be treated as if it counts.

Reading and Writing uses short passages or passage pairs, each followed by one multiple-choice question (four options). Questions appear in a rough order by domain. College Board groups them into four domains: Information and Ideas, Craft and Structure, Expression of Ideas, and Standard English Conventions.

Math includes multiple-choice questions and student-produced response (SPR) "grid-in" questions, where the answer is typed rather than selected — roughly 75% multiple choice and 25% grid-in. Bluebook includes an embedded Desmos calculator and an on-screen reference sheet of common formulas, and students may also use an approved non-CAS handheld calculator. The calculator is allowed on the entire Math section, but algebraic fluency and estimation still matter because some problems are faster by hand.

## Test Requirements and Test-Day Checklist

Knowing the logistics removes avoidable stress and lost points. Always confirm the current details on the official College Board pages, because dates, fees, and policies change.

**Registration.** Create a College Board account, choose a test date and test center, and register before the deadline. In the U.S. the SAT is typically offered in August, October, November, December, March, May, and June. There is a registration fee, and **fee waivers** are available for eligible U.S. students, which can also unlock free score sends and other benefits.

**Accommodations.** Students who need testing accommodations (for example extended time) must apply and be approved through College Board's Services for Students with Disabilities (SSD) well before the test date.

**What to bring on test day:**

- Admission ticket (printed or on your phone) and an acceptable photo ID.
- Your testing device (laptop or tablet) with **Bluebook installed** and **fully charged**, plus a charger.
- An approved calculator if you prefer your own (a calculator is also built into Bluebook).
- Optional: pencil and scratch paper provided at the center, snacks and water for the break.

**Before test day:** install Bluebook, complete the device setup, and run the official practice so the app and tools are familiar. Arrive early for check-in.

**Retakes and superscoring.** The SAT can be retaken. Many colleges **superscore** (combine the best Reading and Writing score and the best Math score across dates), but policies vary, so check each college. Decide on retakes by comparing your score with target-school ranges, not by retaking endlessly.

## What the Score Is Used For

The SAT total score ranges from 400 to 1600. It is the sum of two section scores: Reading and Writing from 200 to 800, and Math from 200 to 800. The score report also includes performance by content area, which helps students identify what to study next.

For applications, the SAT score can serve three practical roles:

1. **Showing academic readiness:** It gives colleges a shared reference point across different schools and grading systems.
2. **Strengthening an application:** A strong score can support the academic side of an application, especially when a student wants another data point beyond grades.
3. **Planning college targets:** Students can compare their score with score ranges published by target colleges and decide whether to retest or focus on courses, essays, and activities.

Testing policies vary by college. Some schools require scores, some are test optional, and some do not consider scores. Students should always check each target college's current admissions page before applying.

## How to Study

My study rule is diagnose first, then train by module, then use full simulations. Random practice too early makes it hard to know whether mistakes come from content gaps, reading errors, timing pressure, or unfamiliarity with the digital testing environment.

### Step 1: Take a Baseline Test

Start with one full official Bluebook practice test. Record the total score, section scores, content-domain performance, and mistake patterns. The first score is not a final judgment. It is a map.

Use four labels for mistakes:

- **Content gap:** The underlying skill was missing, such as quadratic functions, punctuation, or graph interpretation.
- **Question misread:** A condition, unit, or reversed wording was missed.
- **Timing issue:** One question type took too long and hurt later questions.
- **Answer-choice weakness:** Two choices were eliminated, but the final decision was unstable.

### Step 2: Build a Module Checklist

Reading and Writing can be reviewed through the four official domains:

- **Information and Ideas:** Main ideas, details, inference, and information from charts or tables.
- **Craft and Structure:** Vocabulary in context, author's purpose, text structure, and paired-text relationships.
- **Expression of Ideas:** Sentence placement, transitions, concise revision, and paragraph logic.
- **Standard English Conventions:** Punctuation, agreement, pronouns, modifiers, and verb tense.

Math can be reviewed through the four official domains:

- **Algebra:** Linear equations, functions, systems, and inequalities.
- **Advanced Math:** Quadratics, polynomials, exponential expressions, and nonlinear equations.
- **Problem-Solving and Data Analysis:** Ratios, percentages, statistics, probability, and graphs.
- **Geometry and Trigonometry:** Area, volume, circles, angles, and trigonometric relationships.

### Step 3: Use a Weekly Cycle

A practical four-week cycle looks like this:

| Time | Focus | Action |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Monday to Tuesday | Fill skill gaps | Review 1-2 weak topics and complete targeted Khan Academy or question-bank practice |
| Wednesday | Review mistakes | Redo missed questions and write the reason for each error |
| Thursday to Friday | Timed drills | Run 20-30 minute sets to match digital SAT pacing |
| Weekend | Half or full simulation | Take one full Bluebook practice test every two weeks and compare score changes |

If the test is less than two weeks away, reduce new content and focus on common mistakes, pacing, and Bluebook familiarity. In the final few days, do not overload with difficult practice. Prioritize sleep, device setup, admission ticket, and ID.

### Step 4: Set a Target Score and Work Backward

Pick a target total from the published score ranges (usually the middle-50% range) of your target colleges, then split it into Reading and Writing and Math sub-goals. Working backward from the goal keeps practice focused: if Math is the gap, most weekly hours should go to Math until the sub-score moves.

### Step 5: Section Tactics and Common Mistakes

**Reading and Writing**

- Read the question first for grammar/revision items; let the rule (punctuation, agreement, tense) decide, not what "sounds right."
- For inference and main-idea questions, find textual evidence before choosing; eliminate options that are true but unsupported by the passage.
- Watch transitions: decide the logical relationship (contrast, cause, addition) before picking the connector.

**Math**

- Re-read for what is actually asked (value of x vs. x + 3, units, "which of the following").
- Use the built-in Desmos for graphing and solving, but set up the equation first; blind calculator use wastes time.
- For grid-in answers, check format (fractions/decimals) and that the answer fits the entry rules.

**Common mistakes to track:** ignoring the first adaptive module, leaving no time to review flagged questions, and re-doing easy questions instead of the types you actually miss.

## Closing Takeaway

SAT preparation is not about the number of questions completed. It is about whether each practice session answers three questions: what went wrong, how to solve that type faster next time, and whether the same mistake is still repeating. When mistake review and full practice tests form a loop, SAT prep becomes a manageable project instead of passive test grinding.
